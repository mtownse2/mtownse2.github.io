Mortgage Types


Learn about the different types of mortgages and find the one that best suits your needs.


1. Fixed-Rate Mortgage:

A fixed-rate mortgage has an interest rate that remains constant for the entire loan term. This means that your monthly mortgage payments will not change over time, making it easier to budget for the long term. Fixed-rate mortgages are typically available in various term lengths, such as 15, 20, or 30 years.


2. Adjustable-Rate Mortgage (ARM):

An adjustable-rate mortgage has an interest rate that changes periodically, typically after an initial fixed-rate period. The interest rate is often tied to a financial index, such as the U.S. Prime Rate or LIBOR, and adjusts according to market conditions. ARMs typically have lower initial interest rates than fixed-rate mortgages, but the rates can increase or decrease over time, leading to changes in monthly mortgage payments.


3. Interest-Only Mortgage:

An interest-only mortgage allows borrowers to pay only the interest portion of their mortgage for a specified period, usually between 5 to 10 years. After the interest-only period ends, the borrower must begin paying both principal and interest, which leads to significantly higher monthly payments. Interest-only mortgages are typically best suited for borrowers who expect their income to increase in the future or plan to sell the property before the interest-only period ends.


4. FHA Loan:

FHA loans are insured by the Federal Housing Administration (FHA) and are designed to help low- and moderate-income homebuyers purchase a home. These loans typically have lower down payment requirements and more lenient credit score standards compared to conventional loans. However, they require the borrower to pay mortgage insurance premiums, which increases the overall cost of the loan.


5. VA Loan:

VA loans are backed by the Department of Veterans Affairs and are available to eligible veterans, active-duty military members, and some surviving spouses. These loans offer several benefits, including no down payment requirement, no private mortgage insurance (PMI), and competitive interest rates. However, borrowers must meet specific eligibility requirements and pay a VA funding fee, which can be financed into the loan.


6. USDA Loan:

USDA loans are backed by the United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) and are designed for low- to moderate-income homebuyers looking to purchase a home in designated rural areas. These loans offer 100% financing, which means no down payment is required, and they have competitive interest rates. To qualify, borrowers must meet specific income and property location requirements.

Mortgage Rates


Use our mortgage rates to estimate your monthly payments, interest rates, and more.


Mortgage rates are the interest rates that borrowers pay on the principal amount they borrow when obtaining a mortgage loan. These rates vary depending on various factors, such as the type of mortgage, the term length, the borrower's credit score, and current market conditions. There are different types of mortgage rates, which can be categorized as follows:


1. Fixed Rates:

A fixed-rate mortgage has an interest rate that remains constant for the entire term of the loan. This means that your monthly mortgage payments, which include both principal and interest, will not change over time. Fixed-rate mortgages provide stability and predictability for borrowers, as they can budget their housing costs more accurately. Fixed rates are typically higher than the initial rates on adjustable-rate mortgages, but they are less risky as there are no fluctuations in the interest rate over time.


2. Adjustable Rates:

An adjustable-rate mortgage (ARM) has an interest rate that changes periodically, usually after an initial fixed-rate period. The interest rate is tied to a financial index, such as the U.S. Prime Rate or LIBOR, and adjusts according to market conditions. The adjustment frequency can be monthly, quarterly, or annually, depending on the specific terms of the mortgage. ARMs typically have lower initial interest rates compared to fixed-rate mortgages, which can make them more attractive to borrowers. However, the rates can increase or decrease over time, leading to changes in monthly mortgage payments and making them more unpredictable. ARMs usually come with caps that limit how much the interest rate can change during a specific period (e.g., annually) and over the life of the loan. These caps help protect borrowers from extreme fluctuations in interest rates.


3. Discount Rates:

Discount rates are essentially promotional rates offered by lenders to attract new borrowers. These rates are typically lower than the standard mortgage rates for a specified period, usually 1 to 2 years. Once the promotional period ends, the mortgage rate reverts to the standard rate, which could be higher than the initial discounted rate. Borrowers should carefully consider the long-term implications of accepting a discount rate, as their monthly mortgage payments might increase significantly after the promotional period ends.


4. Prime Rates:

Prime rates are the interest rates that banks and financial institutions charge their most creditworthy customers. In the mortgage context, the prime rate often serves as a benchmark for other mortgage rates. For example, adjustable-rate mortgages may be set at a certain percentage above or below the prime rate, depending on the borrower's credit score and other factors. The prime rate is influenced by the federal funds rate, which is set by the Federal Reserve.

Mortgage Differentiation By U.S. Region


Mortgages can differ by regions in the United States due to various factors, including regional economic conditions, local housing markets, regulations, and borrower demographics. Here are some ways mortgages may differ across regions:


1. Interest Rates:

Regional economic conditions can influence interest rates on mortgages. For example, areas with higher economic growth and strong housing demand may experience slightly higher interest rates compared to areas with slower growth or weaker housing markets.


2. Loan Types and Terms:

Different regions may have different preferences for mortgage types and terms. For instance, in areas with high housing costs, borrowers might be more likely to opt for adjustable-rate mortgages (ARMs) with lower initial interest rates or interest-only loans to reduce monthly payments.


3. Down Payment Requirements:

Depending on the local housing market and borrower demographics, some regions may have higher or lower average down payment requirements. For example, areas with high housing prices might see a higher percentage of borrowers utilizing low down payment loan options, such as FHA loans.


4. Government-Backed Loans:

The usage of government-backed loans, like FHA, VA, and USDA loans, may vary by region based on eligibility criteria and borrower demographics. For example, rural regions may see a higher prevalence of USDA loans, while areas with a larger military presence may have more VA loans.


5. Jumbo Loans:

Regions with more expensive housing markets, such as coastal cities, may have a higher percentage of jumbo loans. Jumbo loans exceed the conforming loan limits set by the Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA) and typically have stricter underwriting requirements and higher interest rates.


6. State and Local Regulations:

Mortgage regulations can vary by state, and sometimes even by city or county. These regulations may affect aspects of the mortgage process, such as underwriting standards, disclosure requirements, or licensing for mortgage professionals.


7. Local assistance programs:

Some states or local governments offer housing assistance programs, such as down payment assistance or grants, to help homebuyers afford a mortgage. The availability and eligibility requirements for these programs can vary by region.



Mortgage Process


The mortgage process involves several steps that homebuyers need to complete to secure a loan to purchase a property. Although the process may vary slightly depending on the lender and the borrower's specific situation, the following steps provide a general overview of the mortgage process:


1. Preparation and Research:

Before applying for a mortgage, potential homebuyers should assess their financial situation, including credit score, income, and existing debts. This will help determine their budget and the type of mortgage they can afford. It's essential to research different mortgage products, interest rates, and lenders to find the best fit. At this stage, it's also helpful to gather necessary financial documentation, such as tax returns, pay stubs, and bank statements.


2. Pre-Approval:

Once a homebuyer has a clear understanding of their financial position, they can approach lenders for pre-approval. A pre-approval is a preliminary assessment by a lender of a borrower's ability to obtain a mortgage based on their credit score, income, and debts. It provides an estimate of the loan amount for which the borrower is eligible. Pre-approval is beneficial because it shows sellers that the buyer is a serious and qualified candidate.


3. House Hunting and Making an Offer:

With pre-approval in hand, homebuyers can search for a property that meets their needs and budget. Once they find the desired property, they can make an offer, which typically includes an earnest money deposit and a purchase agreement outlining the terms of the sale. The seller may accept, reject, or counter the offer. Negotiations may take place until both parties agree on the terms.


4. Mortgage Application:

Once the offer is accepted, the homebuyer can formally apply for a mortgage with their chosen lender. The mortgage application involves submitting personal and financial information, as well as details about the property being purchased. The lender will use this information to determine whether to approve the mortgage and under what terms.


5. Processing and Underwriting:

During the processing stage, the lender reviews the mortgage application and verifies the borrower's financial documentation. The underwriter evaluates the borrower's creditworthiness, income, debts, and the property's value to determine if the loan meets the lender's guidelines. The underwriter may request additional information or documentation from the borrower to make their decision.


6. Appraisal and Title Search:

The lender will order an appraisal to assess the property's market value and ensure that it serves as sufficient collateral for the loan. A title search will also be conducted to verify that the seller has a clear and marketable title to the property, free of any liens or encumbrances.


7. Loan Approval and Clearing Conditions:

If the underwriter approves the loan, they may outline specific conditions that need to be met before the loan can be finalized. These conditions may include resolving any issues with the title, obtaining additional documentation, or addressing any discrepancies in the appraisal. Once all conditions are met, the lender will issue a final loan approval.


8. Closing:

The closing, or settlement, is the final step in the mortgage process. It involves signing the loan documents, transferring the funds for the down payment and closing costs, and transferring the property's ownership from the seller to the buyer. Once the closing is complete, the mortgage process is finalized, and the buyer becomes the legal owner of the property.



*Throughout the mortgage process, it's crucial for homebuyers to maintain open communication with their lender, promptly respond to requests for information or documentation, and stay informed about the progress of their loan application. This will help ensure a smooth and timely mortgage process*



Refinancing a Home


Refinancing a home involves taking out a new mortgage to replace the existing one, often to secure more favorable terms or interest rates. There are several options when refinancing a home, depending on your financial goals and circumstances:


1. Rate-and-Term Refinancing:

This is the most common type of refinancing. With rate-and-term refinancing, you replace your existing mortgage with a new one that has a lower interest rate, different loan term, or both. This can help you save on interest payments, reduce your monthly payments, or pay off your mortgage faster.


2. Cash-Out Refinancing:

With cash-out refinancing, you replace your existing mortgage with a new, larger mortgage and receive the difference between the two loans in cash. This option is typically used when you have built significant equity in your home and need funds for other purposes, such as home improvements, debt consolidation, or large expenses. Keep in mind that cash-out refinancing increases your loan balance and may extend your repayment term.


3. Cash-In Refinancing:

In this type of refinancing, you pay down a portion of your mortgage principal when you refinance, reducing your loan balance and potentially securing a lower interest rate or better loan terms. Cash-in refinancing can help you reach the 20% equity threshold, which can eliminate private mortgage insurance (PMI) and lower your monthly payments.


4. Streamlined Refinancing:

Streamlined refinancing is a simplified refinance process offered by some government-backed loans, such as FHA Streamline Refinance, VA Interest Rate Reduction Refinance Loan (IRRRL), or USDA Streamlined Refinance. These programs typically require less documentation and have more lenient qualification requirements. They're designed to help borrowers with existing government-backed loans refinance to a lower interest rate with minimal hassle.


5. Short Refinancing:

A short refinance is an option for borrowers who are underwater on their mortgage, meaning they owe more than their home is worth. In this situation, the lender agrees to reduce the loan balance to match the home's current market value, allowing the borrower to refinance into a new loan with a lower principal balance. However, short refinances are relatively rare and typically require the borrower to demonstrate financial hardship.



Frequently Asked Questions


Get answers to common questions about mortgages and the homebuying process.


1. What is Mortgage?

A mortgage is a loan provided by a financial institution to help you purchase a home. The property you buy serves as collateral for the loan, and you are required to make regular payments over a specified term to repay the loan amount along with interest.


2. What are the main types of mortgages?

There are several types of mortgages, but the main ones are: Fixed-rate mortgages: The interest rate remains the same throughout the loan term. Adjustable-rate mortgages (ARMs): The interest rate can change periodically after an initial fixed-rate period.


3. How much down payment do I need?

The required down payment depends on the mortgage type and the lender. Typically, down payments range from 3% to 20% of the home's purchase price. Some government-backed loans, such as VA loans, may not require a down payment.


4. What is the difference between pre-qualification and pre-approval?

Pre-qualification is an informal process where a lender provides an estimate of how much you may be able to borrow based on your income, debts, and credit score. Pre-approval is a more formal process, where the lender verifies your financial information and issues a letter stating the specific loan amount you are approved for.


5. What is a credit score, and how does it affect my mortgage application?

A credit score is a numerical representation of your creditworthiness, based on your credit history. A higher credit score indicates a lower risk for the lender. A good credit score can increase your chances of mortgage approval and help you secure better interest rates.


6. What are closing costs?

Closing costs are fees and expenses associated with finalizing a mortgage and completing the home buying process. These costs typically include loan origination fees, appraisal fees, title search fees, and other related expenses. Closing costs usually range between 2% and 5% of the home's purchase price.


7. What is private mortgage insurance (PMI)?

Private mortgage insurance (PMI) is an insurance policy that protects the lender in case you default on your mortgage. PMI is typically required if your down payment is less than 20% of the home's purchase price. Once you reach 20% equity in your home, you can request to cancel PMI.


8. How do I choose the right mortgage lender?

Consider factors such as interest rates, loan terms, customer service, and reputation when choosing a mortgage lender. Compare multiple lenders and seek recommendations from friends, family, or real estate professionals to find the best lender for your needs.


9. Can I still buy a home if I have a low credit score?

Yes, you can still buy a home with a low credit score, but you may face challenges in getting approved for a mortgage or securing favorable interest rates. Government-backed loans like FHA loans may have more lenient credit score requirements, making them an option for borrowers with lower credit scores.


10. What is an escrow account?

An escrow account is an account held by a third party, usually your mortgage lender, to pay certain property-related expenses on your behalf, such as property taxes and homeowners insurance. Including these expenses in your monthly mortgage payment ensures they are paid on time and reduces the risk for the lender.
